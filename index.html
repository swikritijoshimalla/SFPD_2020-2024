Welcome to the San Francisco Crime Data Project

This project explores publicly available crime data from the City and County of San Francisco Police Department (SFPD), covering the years 2020 through 2024. The goal is to better understand how crime patterns change over time and across neighborhoods, helping both policymakers and the public make more informed decisions.

Using advanced data analytics, machine learning, and geographic information systems (GIS), the project visualizes trends in San Francisco‚Äôs urban crime landscape. BDA600 Capstone: San Francisco Police Incident Analysis (2020‚Äì2024)

This project is part of the M.S. in Big Data Analytics program at San Diego State University and focuses on San Francisco Police Department (SFPD) incident reports from 2020 to 2024. The analysis explores spatial, temporal, and categorical patterns in crime distribution and develops forecasting models to anticipate short-term changes in daily incident counts across the city.

Project Objectives

  • Analyze temporal trends ‚Üí Examine how incidents vary by hour, day, month, and year.
  • Investigate spatial distribution ‚Üí Map and cluster crime locations by neighborhood and police district.
  • Categorize incidents ‚Üí Identify which incident types and categories are most common.
  • Build predictive insights ‚Üí Use machine learning and time series models to forecast incident patterns and support planning.

Dataset Information

  • File: sfpd_2020_2024_clean.parquet
  • Records: ~611,000 incidents
  • Columns: 14 fields

Key fields include: Incident Category, Incident Subcategory, Incident Date, Incident Time, Latitude, Longitude, Police District, Analysis Neighborhood, as well as derived temporal features such as hour, day of week, month, and year.

Source: Public SFPD incident data portal

Preprocessing Steps:

  • Removed duplicate records and incidents with missing or invalid coordinates.
  • Standardized and converted date/time fields to a consistent format.
  • Engineered temporal features (hour, day of week, month, year) to support trend and seasonality analysis.

Analytical Workflow

  • Data Cleaning & Transformation ‚Üí Using pandas and NumPy to handle missing values, filter records, and create derived features.
  • Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) ‚Üí Descriptive statistics, distributions, missing-data checks, and temporal/spatial visualizations.

  • Visualization ‚Üí Time series plots, heatmaps, and spatial maps built with Matplotlib, Seaborn, Folium, Tableau, and ArcGIS.
  • Modeling ‚Üí Development of time series forecasting models and classification models to support operational insights.

Forecasting Daily Crime Counts

To forecast short-term citywide crime levels, daily incident counts from 2020‚Äì2024 were aggregated into a univariate time series and split into a training set and a 60-day holdout period. Two complementary forecasting approaches were evaluated: Facebook Prophet, a decomposable time series model with built-in support for trend and multiple seasonalities, and a seasonal ARIMA with exogenous regressors (SARIMAX) model configured to capture weekly patterns. Both models aim to reproduce the strong weekly cycle observed in the data and to provide practical forecasts for short-term planning.

Figure 1. SARIMAX forecast vs. actual daily incident counts over the 60-day holdout period, with 95% confidence intervals.

SARIMAX Forecast & Residual Diagnostics

The SARIMAX model was fit using a (1,1,1)(1,1,1,7) specification to explicitly capture weekly seasonality (period 7). Over the 60-day holdout window, the model generally tracked the weekly cycle, but exhibited wider uncertainty bands and higher forecast error compared with Prophet. Residual diagnostics were used to evaluate model adequacy and ensure that most temporal structure was captured by the model.

Figure 2. Histogram of SARIMAX residuals, centered around zero with moderate tails, indicating no major systematic bias.

Figure 3. Autocorrelation function (ACF) of SARIMAX residuals, with most lags within the confidence bounds, suggesting limited remaining serial correlation.

Figure 4. Partial autocorrelation function (PACF) of SARIMAX residuals, indicating that key seasonal structure has been largely captured by the model.

Overall, the SARIMAX residual diagnostics support a reasonable model fit; however, forecasts were more volatile and tended to lag local peaks in the crime series. Prediction intervals were also comparatively wider, reflecting greater uncertainty about future crime levels.

Prophet Forecast & Components

The Prophet model was trained on citywide daily incident counts from 2020‚Äì2024 and used to generate a 30-day forecast, as well as evaluations over the same 60-day holdout period. Prophet produced a smooth forecast curve dominated by weekly seasonality and a mild long-term downward trend. The uncertainty intervals widened gradually with the forecast horizon, consistent with expected behavior in time series forecasting.

Figure 5. Prophet forecast of daily crime counts, showing historic observations, forecasted values, and 95% uncertainty intervals.

A component decomposition of the Prophet model highlights three key elements: a long-term trend, weekly seasonality, and yearly seasonality. These components mirror patterns discovered during exploratory analysis and help explain how crime varies over different time scales.

Figure 6. Prophet trend component showing a gradual rise in incident volume through 2022‚Äì2023 followed by a decline into 2024‚Äì2025.

Figure 7. Weekly seasonality component: higher crime levels on Fridays and Saturdays, with lower levels midweek.

Figure 8. Yearly seasonality component, showing elevated crime during summer and early fall and lower activity in winter months.

Forecast Accuracy: Prophet vs. SARIMAX

Model performance was compared over the same 60-day holdout period using standard error metrics: Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), and Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE).

Model MAE RMSE MAPE
Prophet 21.56 27.83 8.58%
SARIMAX 30.19 37.09 12.58%

Prophet achieved lower error across all three metrics, indicating that it better captured the weekly and yearly periodicities present in San Francisco crime data. SARIMAX produced reasonable forecasts but with higher error and wider uncertainty bands. These quantitative results support selecting Prophet as the primary model for citywide daily crime forecasting in the final dashboard.

Classification Models & Feature Importance

In addition to time series forecasting, classification models were used to analyze incident patterns and to better understand which features most strongly influence predicted crime levels. A tuned Decision Tree classifier was trained using engineered temporal, categorical, and socio-economic features, and its feature importance ranking was examined to interpret the model‚Äôs behavior.

Figure 9. Decision Tree (best grid) ‚Äì Top 20 features, highlighting the importance of lagged incident counts, month, and key incident categories.

Figure 10. Tabular view of feature importances, providing a detailed ranking of temporal, categorical, and socio-economic predictors used in the classification model.

The feature importance analysis confirms that short-term temporal dynamics (lagged counts, month, day of week) and certain incident categories play a major role in predicting crime levels. This complements the forecasting models by offering an interpretable view of which drivers most influence crime fluctuations.

SWOT Analysis: San Francisco Crime Data Analytics Project

A SWOT analysis is a structured way to evaluate this Big Data project by examining its Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. This perspective highlights what the project does well, where limitations exist, what future benefits it can deliver, and what risks must be managed. Looking at all four areas provides a complete view of how effective, scalable, and impactful this crime analytics work can be for the City and County of San Francisco.

This capstone project investigates how crime patterns evolve across San Francisco by integrating five years of incident-level data (2020-2024) with machine learning, time-series forecasting, and geospatial modeling. The goal is to understand when and where crime risk increases, and how temporal cycles, district-level patterns, and routine-activity trends can support fair, data-informed public safety planning.

The analysis combines Python-based machine learning (Decision Trees, SARIMAX, Prophet), ArcGIS spatial processing, and dashboard visualization. Daily crime counts were used to train a Decision Tree classifier that predicts high-incident days, achieving a Macro-F1 score of approximately 0.61 and ROC-AUC ‚âà 0.65. For short-term forecasting, citywide daily totals were modeled using Prophet and SARIMAX, with Prophet obtaining a MAPE of 8.6% and producing stable 30-day forecasts capturing strong weekly and yearly seasonal cycles.

Spatially, incidents were aggregated at the police district level and joined to official district boundaries, enabling district-level comparisons and hotspot identification. These spatial and temporal outputs were integrated into an ArcGIS Dashboard that visualizes district-level risk, historical patterns, and forecast summaries in an interactive and accessible format intended for both public users and decision-makers.

Throughout the project, we emphasized transparency, interpretability, and ethical considerations. While predictive models can support public safety efforts, they also present risks related to bias, data quality, and public perception. Our framework highlights responsible use: predictions are designed to inform community-focused planning, not punitive enforcement. Together, the classification model, forecasting pipeline, and spatial dashboard create a unified, reproducible system for understanding crime dynamics in San Francisco.
